# Patent Non-Assertion Intent

```
POLICY INTENT
NOT YET A LEGAL COVENANT
PENDING PROFESSIONAL REVIEW
```

**Read this first.** This document states what the author *intends*.
It is deliberately **not** drafted as a binding covenant, because a
badly-drafted patent promise is worse than none — it can create
contradictory obligations, interact unpredictably with the existing
MIT licence, and bind successors in ways nobody modelled. Converting
intent into a covenant requires a qualified practitioner.

Nothing here is legal advice, and nothing here grants, extends,
restricts or modifies any licence.

## The intent

The author does not wish to use patents to prevent peaceful
implementation of the coordinate, timing, evidence and assurance
foundation published in this repository.

Specifically, the author does not intend to seek or assert patent
claims over:

- basic mathematics or physical law;
- coordinate, root, frame and epoch specifications;
- uncertainty and covariance propagation methods;
- provenance and evidence-classification schemas;
- the published test vectors and reference tests;
- the negative results and refusal semantics.

## What is deliberately left open

- Commercial hardware, calibration services, integration and support.
- Any genuinely novel physical mechanism, should one ever be
  measured — none has been.
- Defensive positions a practitioner may advise are necessary to
  prevent a third party enclosing the published foundation.

## The disclosure position, stated plainly

This matters more than the intent, because it may already be
decided.

**Versions v3.0.0 through v4.8.1 were published publicly under MIT
between approximately 2026-07-15 and 2026-07-18**, covering the CSCP
coordinate work, PMWR, the R3 root-space resolver and the R4
radix/codec work. See
`docs/v51/RGCS_RELEASE_AND_DISCLOSURE_TIMELINE.md`.

That is a **dated public source release under MIT**. It is *not*
described here as an organised defensive publication, because a
defensive publication is a deliberate enabling disclosure prepared
for a searcher, and that is not what those releases were.

Consequences a practitioner should assess:

1. Public disclosure generally destroys novelty immediately in
   strict-novelty jurisdictions.
2. The US and Canada have limited inventor grace periods, commonly
   twelve months, which on these dates would still be open at the
   time of writing — but conditions differ and this needs prompt
   professional assessment.
3. Material first disclosed in that window may no longer be
   patentable by anyone, including the author.

## Review checklist for counsel

- [ ] Confirm the exact visibility-change timestamp from the GitHub
      account audit log (the repository can only infer it).
- [ ] Determine which subject matter was enabled by the public
      releases versus first disclosed privately in R6/R7/R8.1.
- [ ] Advise whether any grace period applies and when it expires.
- [ ] Advise whether a formal non-assertion covenant, an OIN-style
      commitment, or a defensive publication is the right instrument.
- [ ] Check interaction with the existing MIT grant, which already
      carries an implied patent licence in many analyses.
- [ ] Advise on stewardship transfer to a purpose-bound non-profit.

Until those are answered, this document is a statement of values and
should be read as nothing more.
